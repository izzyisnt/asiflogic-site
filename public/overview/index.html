How it works

Killing cancer with sound, in five steps.

The whole idea in one line: ultrasound tickles a pressure channel, a molecular ratchet keeps score, and only the cells whose ratchet is broken — the cancer cells — die. Here is each step.

1

Ultrasound flicks the channel

A gentle 33 kHz wave nudges Piezo1, a pressure-sensitive pore. It opens only ~1 in 100 nudges, for a moment — a rare, random flicker, not a switch.

the trigger · Piezo1
2

A ratchet keeps score

Those flickers don’t pile up in the calcium (it’s pumped away). They feed a molecular ratchet: a protein (talin) unfolds under pull, and another (vinculin) locks it — one click that can’t slip back.

the integrator · the ratchet
3

Stiff ground makes it click

On stiff tissue the pull builds fast, the ratchet clicks forward and locks, click after click — the cell has now measured and remembered where it is. On soft tissue it slips instead.

reads stiffness · click vs. slip
4

Tpm2.1 is the grip

Tpm2.1 stiffens the actin cable the ratchet pulls on. With it, the ratchet has something to grip. Lose it — as cancer cells do — and the cable buckles: the ratchet freewheels and the cell goes stiffness-blind.

the on-switch · Tpm2.1
5

Only the broken ratchet dies

Under the same ultrasound, a healthy cell’s working ratchet lets it cope and survive. A cancer cell’s freewheeling ratchet can’t — the load it can’t handle tips it into self-destruction. Same beam, opposite fates.

the therapy · selective
