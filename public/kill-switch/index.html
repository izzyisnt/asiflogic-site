The kill switch, molecule by molecule

Two cells under the same beam of ultrasound. Left, a healthy cell with its rigidity sensor intact; right, a cancer cell that has lost it. Every motion below — the channel opening, the calcium flooding in, the sensor engaging or failing, the fate — is played back from the simulation's own frame-by-frame output.

What you're watching. Ultrasound washes over both cells and nudges the pressure-gated channel Piezo1 — but the bare acoustic push is ~10⁵× too weak, so the cell's own myosin supplies the power that swings it open. Calcium (bright) floods in. The healthy cell's talin sensor unfolds, recruits vinculin, and reinforces — buffering the load. The cancer cell's sensor can't engage; the load accumulates until it trips the irreversible commitment and the cell undergoes apoptosis.

Rendered in the crowded, flat-fill idiom of David Goodsell's molecular landscapes, with the cinematic motion of Robert Lue / XVIVO's "The Inner Life of the Cell." Not a literal structure — a stylized cross-section whose timing and outcome are driven by the model's trajectories. Molecular sizes and palette are illustrative.
